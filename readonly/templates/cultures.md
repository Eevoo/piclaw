# Culture template

Use this template when designing a culture. Each section is a cultural universal (a "key"). Below each key are common answer options; pick or adapt options when creating a culture.

---

## Prose template (fill in with sentences)

Replace the machine-readable YAML with short prose lines. For each key: state the chosen option or your custom choice, then write one or two sentences explaining what that choice means in practice and why it developed (environmental pressures, economic needs, historical events, social functions, or symbolic logic).

Example format for each key:

- Key name — Choice: <word or short phrase>. Explanation: <one or two sentences describing what the choice is and why it developed>.

Fill the keys below following that example.

## How to use

- Step 1: For each key above, write a short line using the example format: `Key — Choice: <...>. Explanation: <1–2 sentences>`.
- Step 2: After filling every key, read the explanations together and run a coherence pass: ask how kinship, property, and marriage interact, and whether rituals reflect resource distribution.
- Step 3: Use `Other notes` to capture exceptions, liminal groups, or recent historical changes that complicate simple choices.

## Example (short, prose)

- Name — Choice: Riverlanders. Explanation: A slim riverine society whose life revolves around seasonal floods and communal fish weirs, which shaped social obligations and rituals.

- Summary — Choice: "Riverine, communal, ancestor-feasting". Explanation: Daily life centers on joint management of river resources, with feasts that reinforce lineage ties and manage surplus.

- Age status — Choice: rites-for-adolescents. Explanation: Adolescents undergo water-diving and net-mending rites to prove skill; these rites developed because inheriting and repairing fishing gear requires tested competence.

- Gender roles — Choice: flexible-roles. Explanation: Men and women share fishing and craft tasks because small households need cross-trained members during floods.

- Kin groups — Choice: extended-clan-matrilineal. Explanation: Inheritance of river-house rights follows the mother's line, reflecting historical migration of founding female lineages who managed fish-weed knowledge.

- Marriage — Choice: bridewealth-and-arranged. Explanation: Marriages create alliances between river houses and transfer labor rights; bridewealth ensures mutual investment in complex fishing systems.

- Death rituals — Choice: ancestor-veneration. Explanation: Ancestors are believed to watch floods; memorial feasts ensure they guide seasonal water levels and protect nets.

- Personal names — Choice: names-change-for-events. Explanation: People acquire new names after major life events, signaling status changes and recording social biography.

- Language and symbols — Choice: oral-heavy with river-symbol motifs. Explanation: Low historical literacy and strong ritual performance made oral transmission central; boat and net motifs encode lineage stories.

- Religion / Magical thinking — Choice: ancestor-veneration blended with animist river-spirit rites. Explanation: Water spirits explain unpredictable floods and justify ritual offerings to maintain favorable currents.

- Rites of passage — Choice: elaborate-initiation for fish-weir apprentices. Explanation: Initiations transmit technical knowledge and mark membership in fishing teams.

- Leadership — Choice: council-elders. Explanation: Elders mediate disputes and schedule communal weir repairs because local ecology rewards coordinated maintenance.

- Prestige inequalities — Choice: ranked-chiefs (mild). Explanation: Some elders gain prestige because they control ritual knowledge and surplus distribution, but status remains somewhat open.

- Property — Choice: communal-land with house-rights. Explanation: Riverbanks are held in common but house platforms and weirs are managed by house groups, reflecting shifting river channels.

- Trade / Exchange — Choice: regional-trade-networks. Explanation: Surplus fish and preserved goods circulate seasonally with upriver trading partners.

- Conflict — Choice: mediated-by-elders and ritualized boundary contests. Explanation: Violent escalation is risky around weirs, so disputes are adjudicated with elders and occasional ritual contests.

- Cooperative labor — Choice: communal-labor. Explanation: Building and repairing weirs, and running feasts, require many hands which encourages strong reciprocal obligations.

- Art / Music / Dance — Choice: integral-ceremony. Explanation: Drumming and boat-procession dances coordinate labor and mark seasonal ceremonies.

- Body adornment — Choice: ritual-scars and simple jewelry. Explanation: Scars mark initiation, while jewelry uses river-shells as status tokens.

- Etiquette — Choice: ritualized-greeting and deference to elder knowledge. Explanation: Greetings encode obligations to share surplus and acknowledge lineage rights.

- Proverbs / Oral wisdom — Choice: rich-proverbial-tradition. Explanation: Short sayings encode fishing lore and social rules for sharing catch.

- Color terms — Choice: symbolic-color-system centered on river hues. Explanation: Blues and greens map to water states and tie into ritual clothing for flood-time ceremonies.

- Classifications (knowledge) — Choice: practical-knowledge. Explanation: Taxonomies emphasize fish species, net weave types, and seasonal indicators important for survival.

- Peer groups (non-kin) — Choice: guilds-crafts and age-sets. Explanation: Specialized weir crews and age cohorts organize training and mutual aid.

- Oaths / Promises — Choice: ritual-oaths sworn on shared nets. Explanation: Oaths taken at weir-launch bind people to labor and honest counting of shares.

- Play — Choice: ritualized-competitions. Explanation: Boat races and net-casting contests train skills and release social tensions in structured ways.

- Territory / Mobility — Choice: seasonal-transhumant along river reaches. Explanation: Houses move or are rebuilt as channels shift, but core fishing grounds remain culturally defined.

- Morals — Choice: collectivist-duty. Explanation: Moral language emphasizes duty to the house and sharing surplus during lean seasons.

- Other notes — Choice: recent contact with traders introduced metal hooks. Explanation: New tools changed net design and shifted some labor patterns, creating debates over who controls trade profits.
