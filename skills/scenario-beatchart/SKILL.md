Skill: scenario-beatchart

Purpose:
- Generate a beat chart (ordered list of narrative/gameplay beats) for scenario-style stories or tabletop scenarios.
- Enforce structural rules for hooks, developments, cliffhangers, climax, and resolution and convert a target total playtime into an ordered sequence of beats with suggested durations.

Definitions:
- Beat: a discrete unit representing roughly 0.5 hours (30 minutes) of gameplay or narrative. Most beats should aim for ~0.5 hours of table time.
- Hook: a short, attention-grabbing opening action or suspense that draws players in. Counts as 1.5 hours (3 beats) by default. Hooks are how a scenario gets started and can be:
  - A kidnapping of an NPC
  - A discovery (e.g., a hidden artifact or a corpse)
  - An ongoing crisis that pulls the characters in (e.g., a flood, plague, uprising)
  - A revelation that changes the characters' lives (e.g., secret lineage revealed)
  - Murder or a false accusation against the PCs or an ally
  - A looming threat from an antagonist in the scenario
  - A variation of a Cliffhanger if the scenario starts fast (immediate action)
  - A variation of a Development if the scenario starts slow (investigation, social setup)
- Development: a non-action beat that advances plot or characters (clues, conversations, revelations). Typically ~0.5 hours per beat. Development types and descriptions include:
  - Warning: characters are told of something to come or a previously unknown threat is revealed
  - Hidden weakness: characters learn a previously unknown weakness of the opposition, clearly described so it can be exploited
  - Revelation: a key piece of information is unveiled, letting characters know a larger part of the story; this knowledge changes perception but won't by itself decide the outcome
  - Advantage revealed: characters uncover a previously unknown advantage or tool that can be used later
  - Clue: an ambiguous revelation — one part of the puzzle is revealed but not the whole thing; points the party in a direction without full resolution
  - Retreat: often follows a cliffhanger; opponents withdraw to a stronghold and cover their tracks, delaying pursuit and cooling down immediate action
  - Hesitation: antagonists pause to negotiate, parley, or delay without fully retreating; creates tension and potential diplomacy
  - Mistaken identity: a character is mistaken for someone else (ally or foe), creating complications, opportunities, or subsequent clues
  - Villain's monologue: the opposition's leader boasts about their plan and goals, revealing motives and timelines (can seed future revelations)
  - Secret meeting: arranging and attending a clandestine rendezvous that may yield clues, allies, or betrayals; may be interrupted later by an ambush
  - Personal stakes: the ante is raised when something personal happens to a character (poisoning, kidnapping, injury, family threatened)
  - Second chance: characters get another opportunity to tackle a previously failed problem or rescue attempt; new information or allies may appear
  - New allies: appearance of potential allies who bring new information or resources (may change options without guaranteeing success)
  - Gain mastery: a training/teaching montage where characters learn a new skill or improve abilities relevant to the scenario
  - Alliance: gaining formal help from outside sources; powerful but not decisive on its own unless the scenario's goal is to form the alliance
  - Betrayal: an ally or NPC betrays the party; always allow an opportunity for the players to stop or discover it in time
  - Sabotage: an important device or vehicle of the characters is disabled; players should have a chance to detect, avert, or repair it
  - Foreshadowing: ambiguous hints or omens about events to come (avoid placing this immediately before the event it references)
  - Strange bedfellows: a pressure forces the party to cooperate with former enemies; must be compelling and believable
  - Turnabout: a member of the antagonist faction chooses to aid the party for their own reasons
  - Romance: development that deepens emotional bonds and can create additional stakes or complications
  - Lie revealed: a previously-accepted piece of information is exposed as false; it reveals the deception but not necessarily the full truth
  - Hazardous quest: an arduous objective involving traps, pitfalls, and slogging to find/destroy/recover an important item or location
  - Puzzle: a riddle or problem that must be solved for progress; not a contest against an opponent but a barrier to moving forward
  - Framed: a character is set up for a crime they didn't commit; the frame should be obvious to everyone except the authorities, who hold convincing contrary evidence
  - Obsession: a character becomes the target of another's unhealthy fixation (love, hate, curiosity); the obsessed party will persistently intrude while avoiding direct confrontation
  - Rescuers: a beat where the party must save someone from capture, death, or confinement; motivates urgent action when the target is important to the party
  - Vengeance: an antagonist singles out a character for revenge and deliberately attacks or provokes them, regardless of cost

Additional notes on developments:
  - Warnings and clues often seed the next cliffhanger by providing impetus or a target.
  - Hidden weaknesses and advantages revealed should be explicit enough for players to act on when that moment arrives (e.g., "the ogre is vulnerable to loud noises" or "the relic responds to moonlight").
  - Retreat and hesitation serve as tempo-changers: they reduce immediate tension and give players time to plan, but may also mask further enemy preparation.
  - Mistaken identity and secret meetings are fertile ground for social roleplay and may naturally lead to betrayals or alliances.
  - Personal stakes raise urgency and should be used sparingly to preserve emotional impact.
  - Second chance and gain mastery developments give players agency to recover from setbacks and should be balanced so they don't trivialize earlier failures.
  - Strange bedfellows and turnabout require believable motives — provide reasons that make collaboration plausible.
  - Betrayal scenes should include clues or foreshadowing so attentive players can uncover or prevent them.
  - Sabotage should offer players a detectable trail (e.g., tampered bolts, spilled lubricant) so they can investigate and respond.
  - New allies and alliances should come with costs or strings attached to avoid making them a free power-up.
  - Gain mastery scenes should be compact and focused: grant a single new tool/skill or accelerate an existing one rather than broad, game-breaking power.
  - Lie revealed should challenge assumptions and reopen investigations rather than immediately closing the case.
  - Hazardous quests and puzzles should be paced to avoid turning sessions into grind; provide clear short-term milestones.
  - Framing scenarios must provide a believable trail of planted evidence and an avenue for the party to either discover the frame or produce counter-evidence.
  - Obsession can be a source of both threat and unexpected aid; define the obssessee's motives and limits.
  - Rescuer beats should create moral choices and constraints (time limits, difficult terrain, guarding forces).
  - Vengeance beats are personal and dangerous; balance their intensity so they feel meaningful without derailing the main plot.
- Cliffhanger: an action-oriented beat that creates immediate pressure (fight, chase, timed puzzle). Typically ~0.5 hours per beat. A cliffhanger is a conflict whose outcome is in doubt until the very end. The skill should save the best cliffhanger in a scenario for last. General cliffhanger options include:
  - Chase: the character gives chase, often after a kidnapping or revelation
  - Pursuit: someone is escaping the player (players are pursuing a fleeing or outgunning antagonist)
  - Race: reach a goal first (e.g., reach a shrine, stop a ritual)
  - Fist fight: face-to-face combat, hand-to-hand or melee
  - Dogfight: mounted or vehicular combat where losing the mount/vehicle is dangerous (flying, unstable ground, etc.)
  - Confrontation: non-physical conflict (verbal combat, intimidation, interrogation)
  - Duel: one-on-one fight between two participants with same weapons/skills
  - Battle: full-scale combat where both sides meet openly
  - Ambush: battle where one side is taken unawares
  - Monster: non-intelligent threat (scares, frights, horrors, terrors — graded weakest to strongest)
  - Obstacle: traps, natural hazards, or environmental dangers to physically overcome
  - Contest: ritualized win/lose confrontation (games, riddles, competitions)
  - Skirmish: a small-scale battle where the main bad guy typically escapes
- Climax: the scenario finale where the primary question is resolved or the big confrontation occurs. Counts as 1.5 hours (3 beats) by default. Typical climax types and guidance:
  - Final revelations (mystery reveal): gather the characters and stakeholders in one place to lay out evidence, expose red herrings, and reveal the true facts. The murderer or culprit is identified, conspiracies are exposed, and the underlying mystery for this scenario is resolved. Larger-series mysteries may be teased or made more salient during this beat but need not be fully solved here—treat those as hooks for future scenarios.
    - Structure: allow time for assembled testimonies, presentation of evidence, and player-led accusations. Include an opportunity for the antagonist or culprit to respond, misdirect, or attempt to flee.
    - Mechanics: provide clear clues and contradictions the players can use to refute red herrings; ensure at least one decisive piece of evidence or demonstration that can clinch the case if players find or deploy it.
    - Tone: this should feel like closure; avoid introducing brand-new, insoluble mysteries at the moment of reveal.
  - Final battle (showdown): a large-scale confrontation between protagonists and antagonists, cinematic and decisive. Make this feel grand in stakes and spectacle while keeping mechanics and terrain interesting.
    - Structure: present the battlefield, clear objectives, and constraints (e.g., time limits, civilian safety, environmental hazards). Allow for dramatic moments (heroic sacrifice, turning points) and a final decisive action.
    - Mechanics: scale enemy numbers and threats to be challenging but avoid overwhelming the players; include environmental interactions and chances for tactical play.
    - Tone: the showdown should resolve the immediate conflict; tie its outcome to story consequences and the resolution beat.
- Resolution (denouement): the aftermath and consequences of the scenario that follow the climax. Counts as 1.5 hours (3 beats) by default. Resolution types and guidance:
  - Happy ending: a broadly positive outcome — wounded heal, debts are paid, and relationships mend. Use when the scenario reward is recovery, restitution, or closure.
  - Villain killed: the antagonist is permanently defeated. This is a common closure for life-or-death stakes. Address moral fallout, legal/political consequences, and potential power vacuums.
  - Poetic justice: characters receive consequences that fit their actions (reward or punishment that is thematically appropriate). Poetic justice should feel earned and logically consistent with the scenario's events.
  - Villain surrenders: antagonist yields, is captured, or otherwise gives up and is taken in. This allows for interrogation, legal resolution, and less morally ambiguous closure than killing.
  - Villain escapes: the antagonist avoids capture or appears to have been defeated but slips away. Use sparingly; the escape should be credible (trickery, resources, inside help) and can serve as a hook for future scenarios.
  - Heroes captured: protagonists are taken prisoner or otherwise incapacitated but survive; this is a dark resolution that sets up a follow-up scenario centered on escape/rescue and recovery.
  - Heroes escape: the party survives and evades total defeat but the mission fails or is only partially successful. This is an uncommon but viable resolution that preserves the possibility of redemption later.
  - Greater-threat revelation ("The End??"): the immediate crisis is resolved but a larger, looming danger is revealed or hinted at. Use this to escalate a series arc while still giving partial closure to the current scenario.

Guidelines for writing resolutions:
  - Match tone and stakes: pick a resolution congruent with the scenario's themes and the players' actions. Avoid sudden, unforeshadowed reversals.
  - Honor player agency: outcomes should clearly result from player choices or the information/actions they uncovered; avoid deus ex machina saves unless intentionally theatrical.
  - Foreshadow consequences: plant threads during earlier beats (developments/cliffhangers) that make the chosen resolution plausible and resonant.
  - Use escapes and repeated-villain endings sparingly: allow a particularly prominent antagonist to return once or twice if credible, but avoid overusing escapes to prevent players feeling cheated.
  - Use captured/escaped resolutions to create immediate stakes for the next scenario: define constraints (time, imprisonment conditions, status of allies) that drive the follow-up.
  - Scale the emotional aftermath: allow time in the resolution to mourn losses, celebrate victories, and let NPC and world reactions be visible and consequential.
  - When seeding a series arc (greater-threat reveal), give just enough detail to motivate players without resolving the larger mystery here; treat it as a hook.

Practical notes for the beat chart generator:
  - Genre clarity: if the scenario's overall concept maps to a largely-identifiable genre (thriller, romance, mystery, horror, heist, etc.), that genre should be demonstrated or become clear to players by roughly the first one-third of the scenario beats. Algorithmically: compute first_third_limit = max(1, floor(total_beats/3)). By the beat with index first_third_limit (1-based), the beats and suggested prompts should have established tonal signifiers (stakes, pacing, typical threats or relationship focus) consistent with the declared or inferred genre.
    - Fallbacks: for short scenarios where total_beats is small, ensure the genre is signaled by the end of the Hook and the first Development/Cliffhanger pair (e.g., through an early clue, a tonal scene, or an early moral choice).
    - Action: if the genre is not clear by that point, the generator should either (a) suggest an extra development beat designed to codify genre signifiers (e.g., a romantic reveal, an investigation beat, a tense chase setup), or (b) prompt the author to choose or confirm genre and desired tone.
    - Implementation: accept a genre tag or a genre_bias parameter (0..1) to influence prompt wording, NPC behavior, pacing, and the balance of Development vs Cliffhanger beats during initial beats.
  - Allow the user to request a specific resolution type or accept a recommended one based on scenario tags (e.g., "mystery" → happy ending or villain surrendered; "assault" → villain killed or escape as fits tone).
  - If players' choices in the climax make multiple resolutions plausible, present 1–3 likely outcomes and suggested follow-up hooks for each.
  - If the user requests persistence or scene-writing, include suggested beats/items for the resolution: victims' fates, legal consequences, rewards, hooks for next scenario, and emotional setpieces (funeral, celebration, interrogation).

Core rules (expressed to the agent):
1. Every beat chart MUST begin with one Hook and end with a Climax+Resolution pair.
2. Hook, Climax, and Resolution are special beats that by default occupy 1.5 hours each (i.e., 3 half-hour beats each).
3. Between the Hook and the Climax/Resolution pair, beats are composed of alternating Development and Cliffhanger beats. A Development must be followed by a Cliffhanger, and a Cliffhanger must be followed by a Development, continuing until approaching the Climax.
4. When converting a target total playtime into beats, convert hours to half-hour beats (total_beats = round(total_hours / 0.5)). Then reserve the default beats for Hook/Climax/Resolution (3+3+3 = 9 beats) and distribute the remainder into alternating Development/Cliffhanger beats.

Algorithm (deterministic):
Inputs: total_hours (float), optional story_name (string), optional format ("json" or "md")
1. Compute total_beats = max(1, round(total_hours / 0.5)). (Ensure at least 1 beat.)
2. Default_special = {hook:3, climax:3, resolution:3} (total 9 beats).
3. If total_beats >= 9:
   a. remaining_beats = total_beats - 9
   b. Create sequence: [Hook]
   c. If remaining_beats == 0, immediately append [Climax, Resolution].
   d. Otherwise, append alternating Development and Cliffhanger beats until remaining_beats are assigned. Always start with Development after the Hook.
      - For i from 1..remaining_beats:
        - If i odd -> Development
        - If i even -> Cliffhanger
   e. After assigning remaining beats, append [Climax, Resolution].
4. If total_beats < 9 (short scenarios):
   a. Compute min_special_each = max(1, floor(total_beats / 3)). This guarantees at least 1 beat for each special beat when possible.
   b. Assign hook = min_special_each, climax = min_special_each, resolution = min_special_each. Used_special = hook+climax+resolution.
   c. remaining_beats = total_beats - Used_special. If remaining_beats > 0, assign alternates (Development/Cliffhanger) starting with Development until exhausted.
   d. If total_beats < 3 (fewer than one beat each), fallback to this prioritized minimal sequence: Hook, Climax, Resolution, where some entries may share the same beat index; prefer to keep Hook and Climax separate when possible.
5. For each beat produced, output:
   - index (0-based integer position in the beat sequence)
   - type (hook/development/cliffhanger/climax/resolution)
   - nominal_beats (integer number of 0.5-hr beats assigned to this item; for Development/Cliffhanger usually 1)
   - nominal_minutes = nominal_beats * 30
   - suggested description prompt (one-line prompt the interactive-story skill can use to generate a scene for this beat)
6. Output formats supported:
   - JSON: an ordered array of beat objects (as above); suitable for machine use or saving to readwrite/scenarios/<storyname>/beatchart.json
   - Markdown: human-readable ordered list with timings and prompts; suitable for readwrite/scenarios/<storyname>/beatchart.md

Naming and persistence (optional behavior):
- If a story_name is provided and persistence is enabled, write the beat chart to readwrite/scenarios/<sanitized-storyname>/beatchart/ as beatchart.json and beatchart.md.
- Sanitize story_name: allow letters, digits, hyphen, underscore. Replace spaces with hyphens and remove other characters.
- Create directories as needed. Do not write outside readwrite/ without explicit permission.

Example
- total_hours = 6.0 -> total_beats = 12
  - reserve 9 beats for Hook/Climax/Resolution -> remaining 3
  - sequence: Hook (3), Development (1), Cliffhanger (1), Development (1), Climax (3), Resolution (3)
- total_hours = 3.0 -> total_beats = 6 (<9)
  - min_special_each = floor(6/3)=2 -> Hook(2), Climax(2), Resolution(2), remaining=0 -> sequence: Hook(2), Climax(2), Resolution(2)

Edge-cases and notes
- The agent must ensure alternation: never produce two consecutive Developments or two Cliffhangers in the middle section.
- If the user wants a different balance (e.g., more action-heavy, or longer developments), accept optional parameters: action_bias (float, 0..1 where >0.5 favors more cliffhangers) and scale the assignment of remaining_beats accordingly, while still preserving alternation rules as closely as possible.
- The skill should validate total_hours and ask clarifying questions if the input is missing or ambiguous.
- The skill should be compatible with the interactive-story skill: the beat chart provides suggested prompts that the interactive-story skill will use to write the actual scene files.

Implementation notes for the agent
- When asked to generate a beat chart:
  1. Confirm total_hours and optional story_name (ask for name if persistence is requested and name is missing).
  2. Run the algorithm above to produce an ordered beat list.
  3. Return the beat chart in chat (include both human-readable summary and machine JSON if requested).
  4. If persistence requested, save to readwrite/scenarios/<sanitized-storyname>/beatchart/beatchart.json and beatchart.md and re-open to confirm.
